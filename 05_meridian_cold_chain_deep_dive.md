# Meridian Logistics — Cold Chain Strategy Deep Dive

*Industry Research Brief, Logistics Quarterly, November 2024*

Meridian Logistics has emerged as the dominant cold-chain operator in the Pacific Northwest, a position it has consolidated aggressively over the past 18 months.

## The numbers

Meridian's cold-chain segment generated approximately $680 million in revenue over the trailing twelve months, representing roughly 15% of total company revenue but nearly 28% of operating profit. Segment operating margin of 22.4% is more than double the company-wide average.

The pending $340 million acquisition of Pacific Cold Storage would add 14 facilities, 2.1 million square feet of cold storage capacity, and an estimated $140 million in annual revenue. Post-acquisition, Meridian would control approximately 38% of third-party cold-chain capacity in Washington and Oregon combined.

## The strategy

Meridian's approach has been to build density rather than breadth. Rather than expanding nationally, the company has invested heavily in Pacific Northwest market share, with cold chain as the tip of the spear. The thesis, per CEO Janet Huang's remarks at the September 2024 investor day: "In cold chain, density wins. A customer shipping frozen goods from Seattle to Portland doesn't care about your Texas footprint."

## The competitive implication

For competitors in the Pacific Northwest — notably NorthStar Freight and Apex Supply Chain — Meridian's cold-chain buildout creates pressure on adjacent business lines. Cold-chain customers typically have other logistics needs, and Meridian's density advantage in the region is increasingly translating into wins on non-cold-chain contracts.

Industry sources report that Meridian has won at least four major Pacific Northwest accounts from Apex in 2024, though Meridian has not confirmed specific customer names.

## Risks

The Pacific Cold Storage acquisition faces regulatory review. The combined entity would likely trigger an antitrust review given the concentration of Pacific Northwest cold-chain capacity. Deal lawyers on both sides have privately indicated they expect the deal to close but may require minor divestitures.
